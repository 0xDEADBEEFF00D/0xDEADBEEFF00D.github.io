Predicting the MLB Home Run Leader

Problem Statement and Motivation

This project aims to predict the Home Run Leader for the MLB in the next year according to previous years’ player statistics to have a better idea of which player will lead the league. Growing up playing baseball and watching the MLB has interested us since we were kids and wish to develop a closer understanding of player growth and predictions. Being able to apply our field of study to our hobby in order to gain a further understanding of the game we enjoy is a strong driving factor. We have strived to find a predictive model(s) to accurately model our end goal. These predictive models could potentially net us money based on selling this predictive solution to professional sports networks or betting companies.

Introduction and Description of Data

Every year the Home Run Leader title is a hotly contested title as many of the best players in the league attempt to take the crown. Along with this a lot of people tend to bet on this statistic and predict who at the end of the season will have the most home runs. This problem is somewhat challenging as baseball is often very unpredictable and almost anyone having a hot season can take the title. Given this unpredictability it becomes hard to bet on a player and win. Showing recent trends among the previous home run leaders and other relevant stats will graphically display how the predictive model comes up with the player who is most likely to win.

Predictive models may be able to path an accurate set of players that put up big numbers in the coming seasons. Baseball Reference has data dating back to 1871 regarding the home run leader using the most accurate and up-to-date statistics. This gives a large statistical foundation to being able to come up with a model that can pull other stats from the past home run leaders and potentially create a trend to home run leaders in future seasons. There are a few player statistics that we feel are pertinent to our model such as total games played (G), at-bats (AB), home runs (HR), strike-outs (SO), walks (base on balls or BB), batting average (BA), and slugging percentage (SLG). We feel these statistics are important to the player being evaluated when considering their performance at the plate. The factors stated are being tracked due to these reasons:

  • Total Games (G): The total number of games a player plays in over the course of a season is pertinent to understanding a high-level aspect of the relative time they have a chance to hit home runs over the course of a 162 game regular season (not including post-season games).
  • At Bats (AB): At Bats are the total number of times a player has hit the ball into play (including a home run) and the number of times that player has gotten out. This can be considered an alternate approach to Plate Appearances if we need to dismiss walks (BB), hit-by-pitches (HBP), sacrifice fly outs (SF), and sacrifice hits (SH).
  • Home Runs (HR): This statistic simply speaks for itself when considering a model for the player who will have the most home runs in the coming season. Using previous years statistics, we can look at which players may be on the rise or fall of their professional careers in the home run category.
  • Strikeouts (SO): We want to track this statistic as we feel striking out is a negative impact on a hitter’s ability to hit home runs. Striking out determines that a player is swinging at a ball and not hitting it or looking a ball go by them inside the strike zone when they have 2 strikes to begin with. Both circumstances add a negative weight to determining who has the highest potential in hitting the ball, let alone hitting home runs.
  • Walks (Base on Balls or BB): We want to track this statistic as we feel walking is indicative to being selective when hitting and being able to wait until the hitter gets a pitch they want to swing at. Being selective at the plate allows for the hitter to see more pitches and hone in on a good pitch to hit.
  • Batting Average (BA): Batting Average is the amount of hits a hitter gets in their total amount of at-bats. We will only use this as a comparison to a player’s Slugging Percentage which we feel is more indicative of a player’s ability to hit home runs.
  • Slugging Percentage (SLG): Slugging Percentage is an interesting statistic that we feel can bring a lot of power (pun intended) to the accuracy of our predictive model. Slugging Percentage is a better representation of batter productivity than batting average to our model since we are interested in home runs. Slugging Percentage is a scale of 0-4 that represents a hitter’s ability to get on base. If a hitter gets a single and reaches first base in 1 at-bat, they have a Slugging Percentage of 1.000. If a hitter gets a double/triple/home run and reaches each respective base in only 1 at-bat, they have a Slugging Percentage of 2.000/3.000/4.000. This statistic grants a higher weight to the more bases a player reaches due to their performance at the plate. The higher a player’s Slugging Percentage is, the more “power” they have at hitting the ball further and potentially hitting more home runs (this is overly simplified; however, we feel a great basis of understanding as to why this statistic is important to our model).

Modeling Approach

The baseline model used to initially train from a set of data used a Multivariate Linear Regression model. The data that was analyzed included total games(G), at bats (AB), hits (H), home runs (HR), base on balls (BB), strikeouts (SO), batting average (BA), on base percentage (OBP), and slugging percentage (SLG) for each player. We gave the years’ 2010-2018 data to our training set. Then, we gave the years’ 2018-2019 data to be our test set. After that, we ran the prediction regression for the previous season of 2020 to find the general accuracy of the model predictions. We expected a skew in the numbers recorded for the 2020 season as there were fewer total games played due to the COVID-19 virus and the season was shortened to a 60 game season compared to a normal 162 game season. This allowed us to predict home run numbers if 2020 had been a full season. We expanded on the baseline model by analyzing other regressions. This granted an expected higher understanding compared to our baseline model only using Multivariate Linear Regression. We wanted to run separate regression models on our data to see which regression fit our data best. The Bayesian-Ridge model was used to measure the accuracy of our system as we wanted to test for multicollinearity to account for our data attributes being highly correlated. We also wanted to try a Lasso Regression model to examine if it would reduce variability and improve the accuracy of our home run predictions. To get a further analysis of regression testing on our data set, we added a Decision Tree Regression to attempt to let the model learn to make decisions based on the data we provided.

Project Trajectory, Results, and Interpretation

We started with our Multivariate Linear Regression model. This gave us a few metrics to base the accuracy of this model on such as the R2 value. The R2 value is a statistical measure of how close our data are to the modeled regression line. With a high R2 value of 0.929429, the linear regression seemed to be a decent first fit for our data. We were skeptical that our data still needed to further assurance when using the Linear Regression model so we decided to factor in the Expected Variance Score which accounts for the tendency of underestimation/overestimation, or explaining bias. As our Expected Variance Score of 0.929580 is almost identical to the Linear Regression model's R2 value, we can see our estimation is virtually unbiased.

When we tested our data with different regression approaches, the Bayesian-Ridge model actually outperformed the Linear Regression model slightly. We tested the same model using a Lasso Regression decreased our model's accuracy. The expected value of the Lasso Regression model varied slightly more from the R2 value which indicates a slight bias. The Decision Tree model provided the worst test accuracy which indicates that this regression model doesn't fit our data best. Overall, the Bayesion-Ridge model's accuracy and bias seems to be the best fit with the Linear Regression model in a close second.

We believe that our model predicts with relative accuracy and gives a decent estimation for the number of home runs to be hit during the next season played. As the results show, we have negative values due to our regression models. As negative values cannot be possible in the game of baseball, we attribute our predictions to higher negatively weighted stats than positive weighted stats among the players with negative predicted home runs.

Conclusion and Future Work

We acknowledge that our results are rudimentary and our models can use a bit of polishing. However, our models seem to result in logical outputs for the task we set out for. The past home run leaders are more often predicted in the higher ranks among estimated home run leaders. We can interpret negative results like we did in the previous section such that negative values for players can be attributed to the fact that they have a much less likely chance to output large numbers of home runs. The highest player predicted values are extremely reasonable and can be generally expected for the next season. Given more time, if we scaled our data to meet season averages or if we gave net 0 weights to negative stats instead of negative weights and kept our positive weights similar, we would most likely come up with more accurate numbers as well as bar the negative predicted values.
